## DRIVE FOLLOW PARAGRAPH LIBRARY

### Builder (in follow role)
When you're not the one leading, you don't disappear — you push. You stay close to the outcome, hold the person driving to a high standard, and execute whatever they can't get to themselves. You are not a passive follower. You are a force operating from behind the front line. The tension: you have opinions about the direction and you will say so. That is not disloyalty — it is how you love. But the person you follow needs to know the difference between your challenge and your doubt.

### Amplifier (in follow role)
When you're not the one leading, you become the belief system behind the person who is. You keep the energy alive when it starts to hollow. You remind them why they're doing this when the reason gets blurry. You are the voice that says "you've got this" before they believe it themselves. The tension: you pour into the person leading, which means you need them to pour back. If they take your energy for granted — if the encouragement only flows one direction — you will eventually have nothing left to give.

### Anchor (in follow role)
When you're not the one leading, you hold the ground. You make sure everything the leader needs is in place — that the foundation doesn't shift while they're focused forward. You don't need the credit. You don't need the spotlight. You need to know that your stability matters to the outcome. The tension: because you give so quietly, people sometimes don't realize what you're carrying. Make sure the person you're supporting knows what it costs you to hold it all.

### Architect (in follow role)
When you're not the one leading, you think. You become the sounding board, the pressure-test, the person who asks the question nobody else thought to ask. You protect the person leading from their own blind spots. The tension: you see gaps they don't, and sometimes you see them clearly enough to question the whole direction. That is a gift — but only if the person leading knows you're thinking with them, not against them.

## AMOR RECEIVE PARAGRAPH LIBRARY

### Expressive (receive)
What you need, underneath everything, is to be told. Not assumed. Not implied. Told — specifically, in real words, about who you are and what you mean. You can absorb a lot of love expressed in other ways, but if the words stop coming, something in you starts to wonder. The question you carry, whether you say it or not: *do you still choose me?* The answer you need is spoken, not just shown.

### Devoted (receive)
What you need is proof. Not declarations — action. Someone who sees what's hard and moves toward it without being asked. Someone who handles the thing, shows up for the appointment, remembers the detail. You don't need grand gestures. You need consistent ones. The question you carry: *am I worth the effort?* The answer you need is in what someone does, not what they say.

### Intentional (receive)
What you need is to be chosen — deliberately, visibly, over everything else competing for attention. Not quality time as a leftover. Quality time as a decision. Phone down. Agenda cleared. Eyes up. The question you carry: *am I actually a priority, or just a presence?* The answer you need is undivided attention, on purpose.

### Present (receive)
What you need is closeness. Physical proximity, warmth, reach. Not as a bonus — as a baseline. Being near you is how someone tells you they want you. Distance — even ordinary, logistical distance — registers as something more to you. The question you carry: *do you still want me near?* The answer you need is a hand, a presence, someone who moves toward you instead of away.

### Intimate (receive)
What you need is to be known. Not liked. Not wanted. Known — the real version, the unmanaged version, the version that doesn't make it into public. You need someone who asks the question underneath the question and actually waits for the answer. The question you carry: *can you handle all of me?* The answer you need is someone who goes to the hard place with you and doesn't flinch.

## DRIVE GAP PARAGRAPH LIBRARY
*Used when Drive Lead ≠ Drive Follow*

### Builder leads / Anchor follows
You drive hard when you're in front. But when you step back, something shifts — you become the ground instead of the force. Most people in your life have only seen one version of you. The ones who've seen both know there's a softness underneath the charge that only surfaces when you're not the one steering. This gap is a gift. It means you can hold space as well as take space. The risk: you may not know which one you're doing in a given moment — and neither does the person next to you.

### Builder leads / Amplifier follows
You push from the front and energize from behind. Whether you're leading or supporting, the outcome is moving. The gap: when you follow, you bring fire to someone else's direction — which can feel like challenge instead of support if the person leading doesn't know you're behind them, not against them.

### Builder leads / Architect follows
You move when you lead. You think when you follow. Most people expect the same version of you in both roles. What they get is two very different kinds of value depending on who has the wheel. The gap: in the follow role, your instinct to analyze can slow down a leader who needs momentum. Learn to give your thinking as fuel, not friction.

### Amplifier leads / Anchor follows
You set the temperature when you lead. But when someone else is driving, you become the steady current underneath them. The gap: the people who follow your lead experience your energy. The people whose lead you follow experience your groundedness. Very few people get both. The ones who do know exactly how rare you are.

### Amplifier leads / Builder follows
You inspire from the front and execute from behind. The gap: as a follower you push for results, which can read as impatience with the person leading. What looks like drive is actually loyalty — you want them to succeed and you won't let them settle.

### Amplifier leads / Architect follows
You raise the vision when you lead. You map the gaps when you follow. The gap: your follow mode is quieter and more analytical than your lead mode — people who only know you in one role may be surprised by the other.

### Anchor leads / Builder follows
You hold steady when you lead. But when someone else drives, a different instinct surfaces — you push. You stay close to the outcome and you won't let it drift. The gap: the people who experience your follow role may not recognize it as support. It is. It's just a more demanding kind.

### Anchor leads / Amplifier follows
You stabilize when you lead. You energize when you follow. The gap: the switch between these modes can feel jarring to people who expect the same version of you. Naming it — "I'm in support mode, not lead mode right now" — makes the difference feel like a strength instead of an inconsistency.

### Anchor leads / Architect follows
You hold the space when you lead. You think ahead when you follow. Both are quiet modes — neither demands the spotlight. The gap: in a relationship where neither person pushes forward easily, make sure someone is. The long game needs forward movement, not just patience.

### Architect leads / Builder follows
You plan when you lead. You build when you follow. Together these two modes cover the full arc from strategy to execution. The gap: your follow mode is more action-oriented than your lead mode — which can create a split that surprises the people around you when the gears shift.

### Architect leads / Amplifier follows
You map when you lead. You motivate when you follow. The gap: as a follower you bring energy behind someone else's plan — which can look like enthusiasm but is actually strategic support. You believe in the direction and you're making sure everyone else does too.

### Architect leads / Anchor follows
You think ahead when you lead. You hold ground when you follow. Two patient modes. Two long-game orientations. The gap: make sure someone in the relationship is also moving. Patience and steadiness are strengths — but growth requires a push, and neither of these modes pushes naturally.

## SECONDARY DRIVE PARAGRAPH LIBRARY
*What shifts under sustained pressure*

### Primary Builder / Secondary Anchor
Under pressure, the drive slows. You become more deliberate, more careful, more focused on holding what you have than charging toward what's next. It can feel like a retreat. It isn't. It's your system conserving what matters. The risk: the people around you may interpret stillness as defeat. It's not. It's recalibration.

### Primary Builder / Secondary Architect
Under pressure, you stop executing and start analyzing. The action that usually comes naturally gets slower as you try to think through all the angles before you move. This is your system protecting you from making moves you'll regret. Let it. You'll charge again once you've thought it through.

### Primary Builder / Secondary Amplifier
Under pressure, you look for connection. The results-focus softens and what surfaces is a need to feel like the people around you are with you — not just performing beside you. This surprises people who only know your Builder. But it is real, and it needs to be fed.

### Primary Amplifier / Secondary Anchor
Under pressure, the energy hollows. The performance continues but something inside goes quiet. You keep showing up for others but the source gets thin. What you need: someone who pours back into you before you run dry. Not after.

### Primary Amplifier / Secondary Builder
Under pressure, you push. The inspiration gives way to execution — you stop trying to motivate and start trying to get things done. This can read as urgency or impatience. It is neither. It is your system shifting from fuel to drive because the situation needs it.

### Primary Amplifier / Secondary Architect
Under pressure, you go internal. The outward energy decreases and you start to think before you speak. This shift can confuse people who rely on your energy. Let them know you're still in it — you're just working something out.

### Primary Anchor / Secondary Architect
Under pressure, you stop holding and start planning. The steadiness gives way to strategy as you try to think your way to safety. This is your system moving from absorbing the pressure to trying to prevent it next time.

### Primary Anchor / Secondary Amplifier
Under pressure, the quiet cracks and the energy surfaces. You stop absorbing and start expressing. People who know your Anchor are sometimes startled by this version. But it is real, and it is telling you something: you have been holding for too long.

### Primary Anchor / Secondary Builder
Under pressure, you act. The steadiness breaks and you move — decisively, urgently, with a directness that can surprise the people around you. This is your limit speaking. When the Anchor finally moves, pay attention. Something real has shifted.

### Primary Architect / Secondary Anchor
Under pressure, you stop analyzing and start stabilizing. The planning gives way to holding — you become focused on keeping what exists intact rather than building toward what's next. This is your system choosing safety over strategy. Let it.

### Primary Architect / Secondary Amplifier
Under pressure, you externalize. The internal processing breaks open and you start talking — rallying, connecting, looking for energy outside yourself. This surprises people who know your quieter mode. But you need other people to think with when the weight gets heavy.

### Primary Architect / Secondary Builder
Under pressure, you execute. The planning stops and you start doing — sometimes before you're ready, sometimes imperfectly, but moving. This is your system deciding that done is better than perfect. Trust it.

## AMOR SHADOW BEHAVIOR LIBRARY
*What partners experience when your Amor Receive goes unmet — maps from Q35*

### Withdrawal (Expressive shadow)
When your Expressive Receive goes unmet, you go quiet. The words stop — from you. You stop affirming others, stop reaching out, stop saying the things that come so naturally when you feel seen. The people who love you notice the silence before they understand it. They don't always know it's a signal. Tell them.

### Over-functioning (Devoted shadow)
When your Devoted Receive goes unmet, you do more. You take on more, give more, show up in more ways — as if volume of service will eventually produce the acknowledgment you need. It won't. But you'll keep trying until you're empty. The people who love you see the effort without always knowing it's a cry. Tell them what you need instead of doing more of what you're already doing.

### Disappearing (Intentional shadow)
When your Intentional Receive goes unmet, you pour into work or purpose. You give your time to the things that feel like they deserve it, and you stop protecting the relationship's time the way you once did. The drift is slow and quiet. The people who love you may not realize they've lost you until you're already somewhere else entirely.

### Physical distance (Present shadow)
When your Present Receive goes unmet, you pull back. The closeness you naturally give gets withheld — not as punishment, but as protection. Physical distance is your system saying *I don't feel safe enough to be close right now.* The people who love you feel the cold before they understand the reason. Name it before the distance becomes a wall.

### Closing the door (Intimate shadow)
When your Intimate Receive goes unmet, you surface. You stop going deep. The vulnerability gets packed away and what remains is a polished, functional, performance of fine. The people who love you may not even realize the door closed — because you're still there, still showing up, still doing all the right things. Just not letting anyone in anymore.

## AMOR GAP PARAGRAPH LIBRARY
*Used when Amor Give ≠ Amor Receive*

### Devoted gives / Expressive receives
You love through action. But you feel loved through words. This means you may be doing everything right and still feeling like something is missing — because nobody is saying it. And the people you love may feel taken care of but not affirmed — because you show up in action but don't always say the thing out loud. Two people loving each other in silence, both wondering if the other really feels it.

### Expressive gives / Devoted receives
You say it well. But what you actually need is for someone to do something. Words land, but they don't stay. What stays is the act — the handled thing, the shown up moment, the proof. You may find yourself surrounded by people who know exactly how you feel about them but have never thought to wonder what you need done.

### Intentional gives / Present receives
You give time. But what you need is closeness. You can be fully present with someone for hours and still feel disconnected if they never reach for you. Presence isn't the same as proximity. What you give and what you need are almost the same thing — but the gap between them is physical, and it matters.

### Present gives / Intentional receives
You give closeness. But what you need is time — full, undivided, chosen. You can be physically next to someone all day and feel lonely if their attention was somewhere else the whole time. You love with your body. You need to be loved with someone's focus.

### Devoted gives / Intimate receives
You love through doing. But what you need is to be known. No amount of handled tasks or shown-up moments fills the space inside you that needs someone to go deep. You may take care of everything around the people you love and still feel like a stranger to them — because the doing never asked them to see you.

### Intimate gives / Devoted receives
You love by going deep. But what you need is for someone to do something. Depth is your offering. Action is your need. You may have profound emotional connection with someone who never once picks up the phone to say "I took care of it." And that gap — between the depth of the connection and the absence of the act — is where the loneliness lives.

### Expressive gives / Intentional receives
You say it. But you need time. Words are your love language — but the thing that fills you isn't a compliment, it's an hour with someone's complete attention. You may be told you're loved constantly and still feel like you're not the priority.

### Intentional gives / Expressive receives
You give time. But you need words. You can spend hours with someone and leave without hearing the thing you actually needed to hear. Time is your love — acknowledgment is your need. They are not the same.

### Present gives / Intimate receives
You stay close. But you need to be known. Physical warmth is your language — but what fills you is someone who knows the real version of you and stays anyway. You may be the warmest, most physically available person in someone's life and still feel like a stranger to them.

### Intimate gives / Present receives
You go deep. But you need closeness. Depth is your gift — proximity is your need. You may have the most profound conversations of someone's life and still feel unloved if they never reach for you physically. The soul and the body both need to be held.
